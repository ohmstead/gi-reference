Acute Pancreatitis
Diagnosis · Severity · Management · Complications
Diagnosis — Atlanta Criteria (2 of 3)
CORE ›
  • Characteristic abdominal pain (epigastric, radiating to back)
  • Lipase ≥3× ULN
  • Characteristic imaging findings
IMAGING TIMING CT abdomen/pelvis only if diagnosis uncertain OR failure to improve at 48–72h. Do not image routinely on admission.

Management
CORE ›
IVF LR preferred over NS (lower systemic inflammation). 250–500 mL/hr initially — reassess frequently. Goal-directed resuscitation, not blanket aggressive fluids.
NUTRITION Early oral feeding as tolerated — do NOT NPO unnecessarily. NG/NJ enteral nutrition if unable to tolerate PO. TPN only as last resort.
ANTIBIOTICS No prophylactic antibiotics. Only if infected necrosis confirmed or strongly suspected (gas on CT = pathognomonic; FNA if uncertain).
  • Pain: IV opioids acceptable; NSAIDs if renal function intact
  • No role for early ERCP in pancreatitis alone — only if concurrent cholangitis

Cholecystitis — Tokyo Criteria & Management
EMERGENCY ›
Diagnosis — Tokyo Guidelines
  • Local: Murphy's sign, RUQ mass/pain/tenderness
  • Systemic: fever, ↑CRP, ↑WBC
  • Imaging: US findings (wall thickening ≥4 mm, pericholecystic fluid, sonographic Murphy's)
  • Suspected = 1 local + 1 systemic; Definite = suspected + imaging
Severity Grading (Tokyo)
  • Grade I (Mild): No organ dysfunction, mild local inflammation → abx + early CCY
  • Grade II (Moderate): ↑WBC >18k, palpable mass, duration >72h, marked local inflammation → abx + CCY within 72h or delayed
  • Grade III (Severe): Organ dysfunction (CV, neuro, resp, renal, hepatic, heme) → ICU + percutaneous drainage + delayed CCY
STANDARD TX IV abx (pip-tazo or cefazolin + metronidazole) → laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Grade III: percutaneous cholecystostomy if too sick for surgery.

PSC — Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis
CORE ›
Clinical Features
  • Fibro-inflammatory destruction of intrahepatic and extrahepatic bile ducts
  • Jaundice, pruritis
  • ↑bili, ↑ALP (cholestatic pattern)
  • Strong association with IBD (especially UC)
Risk of Malignancy
  • Cholangiocarcinoma — major concern; annual MRI/MRCP
  • Colorectal cancer (in IBD + PSC patients)
  • Cirrhosis with portal hypertension if untreated
Diagnosis
  • MRCP — "beads on a string" (multifocal strictures and dilations)
  • ERCP if MRCP inconclusive or therapeutic needed
  • AMA negative (unlike PBC)
Treatment
  • No proven medical therapy — ursodiol no longer recommended at high dose
  • PPAR agonists (phase II trials)
  • Dominant stricture → ERCP dilation/stenting
  • Liver transplant — only definitive treatment

Infectious Colitis
CORE ›
Common Pathogens
  • Bacterial: Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, E. coli (ETEC, EHEC), Yersinia, C. diff
  • Viral: CMV (esp. immunocompromised), norovirus
  • Parasitic: Giardia, Cryptosporidium, E. histolytica
STD-Associated Proctitis / Colitis
  • Chlamydia (LGV serovars)
  • Gonorrhea
  • Syphilis
  • HSV, HPV, mpox
  • Ask about sexual history; rectal swabs for PCR
Ischemic Colitis
CORE ›
KEY PATTERN Segmental inflammation on CT/scope — distribution is the diagnostic clue. Ischemic colitis respects vascular territories and spares the rectum (dual blood supply).
Distribution on CT / Scope
  • Splenic flexure — SMA/IMA watershed (most common)
  • Hepatic flexure — SMA territory
  • Rectosigmoid — IMA watershed
  • Right colon — suggests SMA occlusion (more severe)
  • Rectum typically spared (dual supply: IMA + internal iliac)
Risk Factors / Mechanisms
  • Afib / embolic — SMA embolus → acute mesenteric ischemia
  • Hypotension → iHD — low-flow state (post-op, sepsis, shock)
  • CVD / atherosclerosis — chronic mesenteric ischemia
  • Medications: NSAIDs, cocaine, vasopressors
  • Hypercoagulable states → mesenteric venous thrombosis
ACUTE MESENTERIC ISCHEMIA Right colon or pan-colonic ischemia + severe pain out of proportion → emergent CTA + surgical consult

CORE ›
Drug / ClassPattern / Notes

ICIs (checkpoint inhibitors)Immune-mediated colitis — watery/bloody diarrhea; tx: hold ICI, steroids ± infliximab/vedolizumab for severe

Microscopic Colitis
CORE ›
CLINICAL PEARL Normal colonoscopy + chronic non-bloody watery diarrhea = biopsy anyway. Microscopic colitis is a histologic diagnosis — you won't see it on scope.
Types
  • Collagenous colitis — thick subepithelial collagen band (>10 µm)
  • Lymphocytic colitis — ↑intraepithelial lymphocytes (>20/100 epithelial cells)
  • Both: watery, non-bloody diarrhea; normal or near-normal mucosa on scope
Precipitating Drugs
  • NSAIDs
  • PPIs
  • SSRIs
  • Olmesartan
Treatment
  • Discontinue offending drug
  • Budesonide 9 mg/day × 8 weeks (first-line)
  • Bismuth subsalicylate (mild cases)

Diarrhea
Acute vs. Chronic · Mechanism-Based DDx · HIV Considerations
History Clues
CORE ›
Key Questions
  • Duration & onset — acute (<4 wk) vs. chronic (>4 wk)
  • Nocturnal diarrhea — strongly suggests secretory or inflammatory (not functional)
  • Postprandial diarrhea — dumping syndrome, IBS-D, bile acid malabsorption
  • Steatorrhea / greasy stools — malabsorption (EPI, celiac)
  • Bloody stool — infectious, IBD, ischemic, malignancy
  • Weight loss — malabsorption, IBD, malignancy
  • Abdominal pain — character, location, timing
Exposure History
  • Antibiotics — C. diff; always ask
  • Travel / home water source — Giardia, Cryptosporidium, traveler's diarrhea
  • Medications — see mechanism-based DDx
  • Alcohol use — secretory component, EPI
  • Gluten — celiac
  • Immunosuppression — broadens infectious DDx
Mechanism-Based Differential
CORE ›
Osmotic — stops with fasting · stool osmotic gap >125
  • Carbohydrate malabsorption — lactose intolerance, fructose, sorbitol
  • Osmotic laxatives — magnesium, lactulose, PEG
  • Celiac disease (osmotic component)
Secretory — persists with fasting · stool osmotic gap <50
  • Neuroendocrine: VIPoma, gastrinoma (ZES), carcinoid, mastocytosis
  • Bile acid malabsorption — post-cholecystectomy, Crohn's (ileal disease/resection)
  • Ethanol
  • Microscopic colitis (collagenous or lymphocytic)
  • Hypercalcemia
  • Vasculitis, Crohn's (secretory component)
  • Dumping syndrome / bowel resection
Malabsorption — steatorrhea, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, weight loss
  • EPI (exocrine pancreatic insufficiency) — chronic pancreatitis, pancreatic cancer, post-Whipple
  • Celiac disease — villous atrophy, anti-tTG IgA, respond to gluten-free diet
  • Whipple's disease — Tropheryma whipplei; triad: diarrhea, weight loss, arthralgias
  • Short gut syndrome — extensive ileal or small bowel resection
  • SIBO — small intestinal bacterial overgrowth; breath test
  • Chronic mesenteric ischemia
Motility / Dysmotility
  • Scleroderma — gut dysmotility → pseudo-obstruction, SIBO, diarrhea
  • Amyloidosis — autonomic neuropathy → gut dysmotility
  • Sjögren's syndrome
  • Diabetic autonomic neuropathy
  • Post-surgical (vagotomy, Roux-en-Y)
Functional
  • IBS-D — Rome IV criteria; no nocturnal sx, no weight loss, no blood
  • Post-infectious IBS — follows acute gastroenteritis; altered gut motility
Inflammatory / Infectious
  • See Colitis page for full infectious and inflammatory DDx
  • C. diff, GIPP, IBD, microscopic colitis, ischemic colitis

Cirrhosis
Etiology · Decompensation · Volume · Infection · Bleeding · Encephalopathy · Screening
Etiology & Fibrosis
CORE ›
Common Etiologies
  • EtOH — most common in veterans
  • MASLD (metabolic-associated steatotic liver disease)
  • HBV / HCV / HDV
  • AIH — autoimmune hepatitis
  • Hemochromatosis
  • Wilson's disease
Fibrosis Staging
  • F0–F4 by histology (F4 = cirrhosis)
  • FibroScan (transient elastography) — non-invasive dx
Decompensation — Work Up
  • New or worsening ascites, encephalopathy, variceal bleed, or jaundice
  • Ask about: EtOH use, new medications, RUQ pain
  • R/o: infection (SBP, UTI, PNA), progression of disease
Volume — Ascites
CORE ›
FIRST-LINE Sodium restriction + diuretics: furosemide 40 mg + spironolactone 100 mg daily (maintain 2:5 ratio on uptitration). Max: furosemide 160 mg / spironolactone 400 mg.
Diagnostic Paracentesis
  • Send: cell count + diff, culture, albumin, protein, LDH, glucose, Gram stain
  • SBP = PMN ≥ 250 cells/mm³
  • Give 8 g albumin / liter tapped for large-volume paracentesis (>5 L) to prevent post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction
Indications for Paracentesis vs. Diuretics
  • Tense / refractory ascites → large-volume paracentesis + albumin
  • Refractory ascites → consider TIPS or transplant evaluation
Infection — SBP
EMERGENCY ›
SBP DIAGNOSIS Ascitic PMN ≥ 250 cells/mm³ — treat empirically; do not wait for culture. Empiric tx: cefotaxime or ceftriaxone × 5 days + albumin 1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3 (reduces HRS risk).
Workup for Any Suspected Infection
  • CBC, UA/UCx, CXR, blood cultures (RVP if respiratory sx)
  • Diagnostic paracentesis — always tap if new ascites or clinical deterioration
SBP Prophylaxis
IndicationRegimen
Secondary ppx (h/o SBP)Ciprofloxacin or TMP-SMX daily (indefinite)
Primary ppx — high-risk ascitesCiprofloxacin or norfloxacin daily
Primary Ppx Indications (any one)
  • BUN > 25 mg/dL
  • Bilirubin > 3 mg/dL
  • Ascitic protein < 1.5 g/dL (low protein content)
  • Na < 130 mEq/L
Bleeding — Varices
EMERGENCY ›
Variceal Locations
  • Esophageal (most common), gastric, splenic, rectal, portal colopathy
  • ↑INR + ↓platelets = markedly increased bleeding risk in cirrhosis
ACUTE VARICEAL HEMORRHAGE
  • IV PPI BID
  • Octreotide drip (reduces portal pressure)
  • Ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily × 7 days (SBP ppx reduces mortality)
  • Vitamin K if INR elevated
  • EGD within 12h → variceal band ligation
  • Transfuse to Hgb > 7 (restrictive strategy)
